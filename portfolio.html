My Work

3-Axis Gantry Automation

As a manufacturing engineer at Joby Aviation, I automated a material dispense process using a 3-axis gantry robot in order to improve mass and path consistency. I taught points on a teach pendant, conducted dispense trials to evaluate path settings, and wrote a machine operation procedure for process handover to technicians. This process was tested and validated.

The stock photos included here are meant to represent visually the robot that I brought up within IP constraints.

Sleeve Holding Crate

As a manufacturing engineer at Joby Aviation, I designed stackable sheet metal crates for holding parts during storage, ultrasonic washing, and oven drying. These crates are economical to buy in bulk, easily handled by production technicians and associates, and provide a convenient container for parts from the inventory rack to the assembly station. I created the sheet metal and machined aluminum designs in SolidWorks, prototyped the tool with waterjet cutting, and ordered final versions from a machining/sheet metal shop.

The images included here are meant to represent visually the crate that I designed, some possible shapes for parts to be washed, and the type of equipment it is used with. I've withheld images of the true design, parts, and ultrasonic washer to maintain IP constraints.

Force-Measuring Press Head

One problem I needed to solve at Joby was the integration of a force measurement feature into a standard arbor press. I was tasked with implementing a measurement feature while maintaining maximum press travel (i.e., taking up minimum vertical space). I routed the chosen sensor to an LCD display to show live press force data.

These stock images show similar arbor presses, load cells, and displays. Custom machined parts have been omitted per company IP guidelines.

Pneumatic Silicone Effector

Stanford MSME soft robotics group final project. With my group, I created a pneumatically actuated four-chamber silicone effector capable of bending in any lateral direction away from its longitudinal axis. We cast the effectors in 3D-printed PLA molds, and sealed in pneumatic interface tubes with silicone glue. We tested single- and multi-chamber actuation successfully and recorded bend angle data against applied pressure. Further data collection could allow for direct translation of desired bend angle / end position to applied pressure.

Process Automation Control Box

As an intern at Neptune Medical, I laid out, assembled, wired, and tested the internal systems of a control box for a custom process machine used by their Cardiovascular and Gastrointestinal teams. Working from diagrams and a bill of materials created by the lead engineer of the project, I created state diagrams, fluid diagrams, and part layouts. During assembly, I implemented mounting methods and routed wires, external cables, and pneumatic tubes, as reviewed and approved by the lead engineer. I tested all high voltage and logic-level hardware. I also attached wheels and hardware to the box enclosure for ease of transport. My state and fluid diagrams were shipped out to a consultant to inform control box programming.

Though the royalty-free photos attached here are not the panel that I worked on, they are representative of the type of panel that I built.

Tube Holding Clip For Aluminum Extrusion Machine

As an intern at Neptune Medical, I designed a holding clip for retaining tubes within a custom process machine made of T-slot aluminum extrusion. This clips solved a process issue in which tubes covered in solution were otherwise left loose by operating engineers. I created multiple iterations using 3D-printed resin components and custom-cut EPTFE, tested each iteration with representative tubing, and revised designs based on performance and feedback from machine users.

The stock photos here are meant to represent the processes and hardware used for this project while protecting company information.

SLM Aluminum Roll Cage

Team capstone project for Stanford BSME, for use in a World Solar Challenge electric vehicle. The design objective for the roll cage was to provide adequate head protection for the driver in the event of various crash scenarios while maintaining the lowest roll cage mass possible. I conducted generative design studies in Fusion 360, helped choose an optimal design from the generated options, and simulated the roll cage subject to load cases of [-4g, 1.5g, -5g] and 5g in any direction. The roll cage was fabricated out of aluminum by Uniformity Labs using SLM additive manufacturing.

High-Voltage Battery Enclosure and Modules

I designed a fiberglass enclosure for an electric vehicle battery pack and led the Stanford Solar Car Project Battery Team through enclosure manufacturing and assembly (using CNC routing and DP460 epoxy). I also designed battery modules containing two p-groups of nine cells each, and led the SSCP Battery Team through manufacturing and assembling those modules as well. Though I did not remain with SSCP through the end of battery construction, I created full CAD models of the finished battery pack and developed repeatable procedures to manufacture more modules and enclosure pieces from the same design.

Silver and Bronze Jewelry

I created pieces of silver and bronze jewelry using piercing, soldering, and investment casting. Pieces shown here include a cuttlefish-eye silver pendant, an insect-inspired silver ring, and a bronze piece designed around the concept of tension. Investment cast parts were made in wax before being cast in bronze or silver, and pierced parts were directly manufactured in silver. All parts were sanded and polished to high luster, and were presented in Stanford's Winter ME298/Silversmithing Showcase.

Miniature-Scale Basketball Robot

My team and I built a robot to play miniature-scale basketball for the Stanford ME218B (mechatronics) final project. The robot delivered balls to the basket using a flywheel-based launching mechanism, and was loaded a single ball at a time using a servo-based magazine-style loading mechanism. The robot was programmed using three PIC32 ICs communicating over SPI. I designed and built the loading mechanism, constructed parts of the launching mechanism, soldered motor control and power distribution boards, and wrote services for the project codebase. Project website can be found at buckets32.weebly.com.

SPI Arcade Machine

My team and I built an arcade machine for a jetpack target shooting game for the Stanford ME218A (mechatronics) final project. This arcade machine accepted analog input from a joystick and digital inputs from a push-button and a Hall effect sensor, and featured responsive light and fan feedback to player input. The machine also featured a servo-based game clock display. The arcade machine was programmed using one PIC32 IC and a buffered dot matrix display. I designed schematics, wired circuits, manufactured enclosure components, wrote services, tested hardware and software, and created pixel art. Project website can be found at spijetpack.weebly.com.

Vehicle Controller

My team and I created two controllers designed to drive a full-size test vehicle for the Stanford ME227 (vehicle dynamics) final project. We created a lookahead controller and a PID controller, both of which were successfully run on the Stanford CARS Lab test vehicle. I wrote and revised sections of the control algorithms, tuned gains, and ran simulations to evaluate controller performance. The image at right was taken from the streamed test of our controllers, broadcast on twitch.tv/letsdothemath.
