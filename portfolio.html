My Work

Coater Automation Control Box

I laid out, assembled, wired, and tested the internal systems of a control box for a custom coating machine used by Neptune Medical's Cardiovascular and Gastrointestinal teams. Working from diagrams and a bill of materials created by the lead engineer of the project, I created part layouts, implemented mounting methods, and routed wires, external cables, and pneumatic tubes. I also attached caster wheels, stands, and a handle to the box enclosure for ease of transport.

I used Microsoft Visio to create 2D layouts of the control box panel, reviewed designs with the the lead engineer, and chose an appropriate layout. I mounted all hardware with tapped holes and screws, zip ties, or both. I mounted the Intelligent Power Center to a separate aluminum plate, allowing for easy power supply replacement.

I wired all high-voltage and logic-level DIN rail hardware, including a safety circuit with an emergency stop switch and a custom length 120VAC cable. I tested each stage of the safety circuit, probed for connections between live, neutral, and ground, tested each high-voltage relay, and confirmed that each pneumatic solenoid valve fired when activated.

Tube Holding Clip For Coating Machine

I designed a clip to hold two tubes within a custom coating machine for Neptune Medical. The issue presented involved two solution-covered tubes being left loose during the current coating procedure, and the operating engineers asked for clips to be attached to 8020 rails in order to hold the loose tubes. I created multiple iterations using 3D-printed FORM resin components and custom-cut EPTFE, tested each iteration using 1/8" tubing, and revised designs based on performance and feedback from the engineers using the coating machine. The final clip iteration holds the tube in two locations for better stability, with EPTFE pieces held on by screws threaded into the 3D-printed base.

SLM Aluminum Roll Cage

Team capstone project for Stanford BSME, for use in a World Solar Challenge electric vehicle. The design objective for the roll cage was to provide adequate head protection for the driver in the event of various crash scenarios while maintaining the lowest roll cage mass possible. I conducted generative design studies in Fusion 360, helped choose an optimal design from the generated options, and simulated the roll cage subject to load cases of [-4g, 1.5g, -5g] and 5g in any direction. The roll cage was manufactured out of aluminum by Uniformity Labs using SLM additive manufacturing.

High-Voltage Battery Enclosure and Modules

I designed a fiberglass enclosure for an electric vehicle battery pack and led the Stanford Solar Car Project Battery Team through enclosure manufacturing and assembly (using CNC routing and DP460 epoxy). I also designed battery modules containing two p-groups of nine cells each, and led the SSCP Battery Team through manufacturing and assembling those modules as well. Though I did not remain with SSCP through the end of battery construction, I created full CAD models of the finished battery pack and developed repeatable procedures to manufacture more modules and enclosure pieces from the same design.

Silver and Bronze Jewelry

I created pieces of silver and bronze jewelry using piercing, soldering, and investment casting. Pieces shown here include a cuttlefish-eye silver pendant, an insect-inspired silver ring, and a bronze piece designed around the concept of tension. Investment cast parts were made in wax before being cast in bronze or silver, and pierced parts were directly manufactured in silver. All parts were sanded and polished to high luster, and were presented in Stanford's Winter ME298/Silversmithing Showcase.

Miniature-Scale Basketball Robot

My team and I built a robot to play miniature-scale basketball for the Stanford ME218B (mechatronics) final project. The robot delivered balls to the basket using a flywheel-based launching mechanism, and was loaded a single ball at a time using a servo-based magazine-style loading mechanism. The robot was programmed using three PIC32 ICs communicating over SPI. I designed and built the loading mechanism, constructed parts of the launching mechanism, soldered motor control and power distribution boards, and wrote services for the project codebase. Project website can be found at buckets32.weebly.com.

SPI Arcade Machine

My team and I built an arcade machine for a jetpack target shooting game for the Stanford ME218A (mechatronics) final project. This arcade machine accepted analog input from a joystick and digital inputs from a push-button and a Hall effect sensor, and featured responsive light and fan feedback to player input. The machine also featured a servo-based game clock display. The arcade machine was programmed using one PIC32 IC and a buffered dot matrix display. I designed schematics, wired circuits, manufactured enclosure components, wrote services, tested hardware and software, and created pixel art. Project website can be found at spijetpack.weebly.com.

Vehicle Controller

My team and I created two controllers designed to drive a full-size test vehicle for the Stanford ME227 (vehicle dynamics) final project. We created a lookahead controller and a PID controller, both of which were successfully run on the Stanford CARS Lab test vehicle. I wrote and revised sections of the control algorithms, tuned gains, and ran simulations to evaluate controller performance.
